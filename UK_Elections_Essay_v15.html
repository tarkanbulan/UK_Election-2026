Introduction

Predicting elections has never been easy. Traditional polls and surveys can struggle with low response rates, voters who keep their views private, and last-minute changes of mind. They tend to capture the surface of public opinion rather than the structure beneath it. In 2026, with public trust in politics under strain and new parties reshaping the political map, that surface view looks particularly fragile.

TarCo has built a different kind of model. Instead of asking only "Who wins?", it asks "How governable will England be once the votes are counted?" Using only publicly available data, and drawing on a wide range of expertise including defence research, forensic intelligence, audit, and economics, the framework is focused on one simple but powerful idea: No Overall Control, or NOC.

For the 107 English councils up for election, the model's central estimate is that around 63.22% could end up with no single party in overall control. In other words, fragmentation is not a remote tail risk — it is close to the expected outcome.

Emotion, Empathy, and the Edges

Part of this dynamic is emotional, not just arithmetic. In several urban areas at the edges of the political map — including communities where distressing international events have stirred deep concern — political behaviour is shaped by more than day-to-day local services.

How heightened public feeling affects electoral outcomes

When emotional signals are very strong, people of any community tend to process information through a lens of worry, anger, or wounded dignity. Slower, more reflective thinking is still present, but it can be pushed into the background for a time.

Translated into elections, a more emotionally charged climate can produce:

  • A break with longstanding party loyalties, sometimes quite suddenly.
  • Less patience with compromise or with "business as usual".
  • A preference for political voices that signal moral clarity over administrative competence.

In the TarCo model, councils such as Birmingham and Manchester are not described as "manipulated" zones, and no community is singled out for blame. They are treated as councils where heightened public feeling meets long-standing income gaps and demographic patterns — an environment where the chances of Labour losing ground and councils ending in NOC rise without any assumption of bad faith on the part of voters.

People who feel deeply about distant suffering, or about hardship at home, are exercising a normal democratic right when they let those feelings shape their vote.

Fragmentation and the "Golden Triangle"

If most councils drift into NOC, fragile local coalitions will not be a curiosity — they will be the everyday operating environment. High levels of NOC, combined with services that are already stretched, can create three risks:

RiskDescription
Decision-making gridlockCouncils where no group can sustain a working majority stall on key decisions.
Service disruptionBudget or staffing decisions become hostage to constant negotiation.
Trust erosionLocal government appears stuck in low-level conflict rather than problem-solving.

A stabilising response by central actors — government, oversight bodies, and key service providers — becomes almost inevitable in such a scenario. This is a practical claim, not a conspiratorial one. Such a response would mean clearer frameworks for cross-party arrangements, basic safeguards for essential services, and more honest public communication about the trade-offs that come with fragmented control.

It is an attempt to manage a difficult landing, not to prevent pluralism. Pluralism, with all its noise and disagreement, remains the point of the exercise.

A Different Question

The value of this work does not lie in any single, elegant percentage. It lies in shifting the question being asked. Most public debate will focus on who has gained or lost how many seats. This model invites a different question:

The central question

After the votes are counted, how many of our councils will still be straightforwardly governable?

If the answer is close to the model's 63% NOC estimate, then the 2026 local elections will be remembered less for who "won" and more for how far the system itself moved towards permanent negotiation. In that world, the real contest is not only between parties. It is between fragmentation and the shared capacity to keep governing well — for every community, of every faith, in every part of the country.
