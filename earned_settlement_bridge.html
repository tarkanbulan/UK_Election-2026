1. Two Legal Categories That Must Not Be Conflated

The first disciplinary act of any serious reading is to separate the two propositions the public discussion routinely confuses. They occupy different legal universes, are delivered by different mechanisms, and produce different — though overlapping — damage.

Earned Settlement (10-year rule)

Target: People on limited leave (Skilled Worker, Health & Care, partner route) currently progressing towards ILR under the 5-year framework.

Mechanism: Statement of Changes to the Immigration Rules — an HC paper laid before Parliament.

Parliamentary route: Negative resolution under s.3(2) Immigration Act 1971. No affirmative vote required.

Cohort scale: Approximately 1.9–2.2 million in-flight visa holders and dependants.

Verdict: Legally feasible. Vulnerable to judicial review on specific grounds.

Mass ILR Revocation (Reform claim)

Target: People who already hold ILR or British citizenship — a vested legal status.

Mechanism: Would require primary legislation, ECHR withdrawal, and overhaul of Withdrawal Agreement obligations.

Parliamentary route: Full Bill, both Houses, judicial review survival, and Strasbourg exposure.

Cohort scale: Approximately 5 million plus ILR and naturalised citizens.

Verdict: Structurally impossible. Covered in the Legal Impossibility Bridge.

The Decisive Insight
The government does not need Reform's legislation to inflict most of the damage Reform's rhetoric implies. Earned Settlement, applied retrospectively, achieves a large fraction of the demographic and economic effect through a Statutory Instrument route that does not even reach the floor of the House of Commons.

2. The Decree Mechanism — How It Actually Works

Under section 3(2) of the Immigration Act 1971, the Secretary of State lays a Statement of Changes to the Immigration Rules before Parliament as an HC paper. The "negative resolution" procedure means: the Rules take effect on a specified date — typically 21 to 28 days after laying; Parliament may pray against the changes within 40 sitting days; a prayer motion requires opposition time and a vote, which is almost never granted and almost never successful; the Rules become operative law without any affirmative vote.

This is how every major Immigration Rule change has been made since 1971 — the £18,600 spouse income threshold, the Health & Care visa creation, the Rwanda framework, the BNO route. No primary legislation. No parliamentary vote. No referendum. The constraints on this power are therefore not parliamentary. They are judicial.

3. Retrospective Effect on Skilled Worker Holders — Four Pressure Points

The government will frame Earned Settlement as prospective in form — "We are not stripping anyone's ILR; we are changing future eligibility criteria." This framing has carried weight in past Immigration Rule changes. It is, however, retrospective in substance when applied to current visa holders. The legal pressure points are four.

Pressure Point 1

Legitimate Expectation

Skilled Worker holders entered on a published, relied-upon framework. They ordered their lives — mortgages, NHS surcharges paid in advance, school enrolment, pension vesting — around 5-year settlement.

R (BAPIO Action Ltd) v SSHD [2007] EWHC 199 (Admin); Pantellerisco line.

Pressure Point 2

Article 8 Proportionality

Doubling the settlement route is a substantial interference with private and family life. Proportionality assessment required individually and structurally.

Hesham Ali v SSHD [2016] UKSC 60; Agyarko [2017] UKSC 11.

Pressure Point 3

Children's Welfare Duty

Children who entered as dependants under the 5-year framework now face 10 years of conditional status. Schooling, healthcare entitlement, and university fee status all destabilised.

Section 55, Borders Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009; ZH (Tanzania) [2011] UKSC 4.

Pressure Point 4

Consultation Duty (Gunning)

Consultation closed 12 February 2026. Government must "have regard" to responses. Sept/Oct timing tied to electoral signal, not consultation engagement, is a clean judicial review ground.

R v Brent LBC, ex p Gunning (1985); R (Moseley) v Haringey [2014] UKSC 56.

Forensic judgement: Earned Settlement applied retrospectively to in-flight Skilled Worker holders is legally vulnerable but not impossible. The government's deferral to September/October — to read the May election signal — itself constitutes evidence of political risk-pricing, and may be deployed in judicial review proceedings to demonstrate that the policy is driven by perceived political mandate rather than careful regard for legitimate expectation.

4. The Family Asymmetry — The Constitutional Weakest Link

The 5-versus-15 year spousal asymmetry the consultation contemplates is, in our forensic reading, the most exposed point of the entire proposal. Where one spouse qualifies for British citizenship in 5 years under one route while the other spouse on Skilled Worker faces 10 years to ILR plus 1 year of residency for naturalisation — and where the children's status is undetermined — the policy creates a constitutional configuration that the Supreme Court has previously refused to permit.

The Supreme Court in MM (Lebanon) [2017] UKSC 10 partially struck down the £18,600 income threshold for spouse visas on closely related grounds. The same logic applies, with greater force, to the family asymmetry contemplated here. The question is not whether this component falls, but how much damage it does in the 18–30 months before the Supreme Court rules.

5. The Skilled Worker Cohort — Why the 2 Million Number Matters

The in-flight cohort, in approximate terms drawn from UK government statistics for the year ending September 2025, comprises Skilled Worker holders (~370,000 primary, ~280,000 dependants), Health and Care visa holders (~140,000 primary, ~200,000 dependants), partner-route progressing to ILR (~280,000), Graduate route in transition (~135,000), and other progressing routes (~290,000). The total exposed cohort is in the order of 1.9 to 2.2 million people, of whom approximately 45 to 55 per cent are NHS-relevant.

6. Reform's Deportation Claim — Why Even Reform Does Not Believe It

The Captain's observation deserves formal record. Reform's leadership do not believe their own ILR-deportation claim for three observable reasons.

Observable Tell 1

No Legal Mechanism Published

No White Paper. No draft Bill clause. No ECHR-withdrawal sequencing. Genuine policy intent produces drafted instruments. Performative rhetoric does not.

Observable Tell 2

No Operational Costing

Removing even 100,000 ILR holders requires detention capacity, charter flights, bilateral readmission agreements, and a multi-year legal pipeline. Reform's published costings do not reach this question.

Observable Tell 3

Coalition Behaviour

In coalition discussions and council formation, Reform behaves as politicians who expect to govern. Politicians who expect to govern do not legislate impossible things — they legislate damaging things that survive judicial review.

The Reading

The Cover Function

Reform's deportation rhetoric is the political cover under which a Conservative or Reform-influenced government implements Earned Settlement retrospectively. The rhetoric makes the Statutory Instrument feel like a moderate compromise.

The Forensic Bottom Line

The legally impossible threat — mass ILR revocation — is providing political cover for a legally feasible but constitutionally vulnerable retrospective change — Earned Settlement — that, applied to roughly 2 million in-flight Skilled Worker holders and their families, will deliver most of the demographic and fiscal damage of the impossible threat, without ever reaching the floor of the House of Commons.
