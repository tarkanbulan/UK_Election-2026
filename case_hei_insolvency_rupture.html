1. The Financial Fracture: Revenue Inversion

The UK HEI model was built on a "Volume-based Cross-Subsidy" (VCS). Domestic undergraduate fees, frozen at £9,250 (inflation-adjusted value ~£6,500), represent a structural loss. This loss was covered by unregulated international fees. The new policy regime has inverted this relationship.

The £925 Levy Impact

The flat-rate levy on international student enrollments (starting Aug 2028) acts as a direct tax on the sector's only profitable revenue stream. TarCo modeling indicates that for Tier-2 and Tier-3 universities, the levy eliminates the net margin per student once recruitment costs are factored in.

The Insolvency Vector

24 institutions have been identified in the "Amber Zone" for liquidity. 45% of English providers are projected to report a deficit for 2025-26. Unlike typical corporate bankruptcy, a university failure creates a "Social Vacuum" in regional economies like the Midlands and the North.

3. The "Earned Settlement" Barrier

The proposed change from 5 to 10 years for Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR) is the single most destructive psychological factor in global recruitment. High-value talent (STEM/AI) is now actively pivoting to Canada and Australia, where the path to stability is clear and legislatively stable.

Forensic Note: The requirement for **Primary Legislation** means that even if the government announces the change, it may never pass through a fragmented House of Commons. This creates a state of permanent "Strategic Uncertainty" — the worst possible environment for long-term institutional planning.

4. Conclusions: The Post-HEI Era

The UK Higher Education Industry is the next foundation layer to fail. The model indicates that without an immediate re-baselining of domestic fees or a reversal of the 10-year rule, the UK will lose its "Tier 1" status as a global educational hub by 2029. The "Knowledge Economy" will be replaced by a "Knowledge Deficit".
